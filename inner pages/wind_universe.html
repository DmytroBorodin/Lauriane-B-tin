Wind Universe

2018 - 2019

L’École de design Nantes Atlantique. End of studies project

Resilience of coastal people in face of storms.

L’École de design Nantes Atlantique was the school where I did my Bachelor’s and Master’s Degree. To complete this last one, I worked for almost ten months on my end-of-studies project and my thesis. Having lived in Brittany for most of my life and being surrounded by the sea, I chose to work on the wind and its impacts on our lives. The city that I will be studying in this research is Lesconil, a little town which is part of Plobannalec-Lesconil municipality in Brittany. I decided to work on Lesconil because it is particularly exposed to south west winds coming from the Atlantique. This end-of-studies project led me to step outside of my comfort zone, by interviewing habitants, by creating design tools to better understand their knowledge in face of wind, by finding experts and asking them questions, by reading a lot of articles to become imbued with my subject.

Context and stakes.

01. Context

In today’s world, storms are more and more violent, but do populations know this fact? What do they do to be resilient? How do they act when a storm is coming? And when it is hitting? My reading and interviews with experts allowed me to expose some important facts:

  • Storms are more violent because of climate change.
  • They are very hard to forecast, hence their dangerousness.
  • Mid latitudes (where France is located) are more exposed to violent winds.
  • Brittany is a more exposed area because of the south west winds coming from the Atlantique, so its coastlines are very fragile and subject to erosion and flooding.
  • Lesconil is one of the most exposed zone of Brittany (without counting the islands) because it is located in the south with no direct obstacle. Moreover, having a seasonal public increase, a lot of people need to be aware of storms and its dangers.

Lesconil.

I chose Lesconil because I have a holiday home in this city and used to spend all my summer there. To do a research work and to get information, knowing the city and surrounding areas is an advantage. Moreover, this small town is dear to my heart because of its people, history and culture, so I wanted to honour it.

Located near the sea, Lesconil is a small marina in south Finistère, in Brittany. Its main activity is tourism and for this purpose, a lot of bars and restaurants are located around the port. It also has different beaches on both sides of the port, and different promenades are available to visit the coastlines and the surrounding areas. Based on the south coastlines, Lesconil is often exposed to violent winds coming from the south west.

Phase 1. Research.

Doing research, following trends and looking continuously at the new projects, is a daily life for a designer. Always being curious and keen to learn more and more every day, no matter the topic. It will allow me to collect data, set up a context and understand the stakes of my subject.

01. Collecting data

It is very important to collect data and have different kinds of sources. I read a lot of online articles and newspapers, studied some books and questioned a lot of people. In Lesconil, I interviewed about twenty random persons, but also the tourist office, the Mayor of Lesconil, the town hall urbanist, the sailing center, the local bars and restaurants. I also interviewed other people outside Lesconil, to have tourists’ opinions and experts’ points of view. I created a survey for the tourists and called, mailed or interviewed in person some experts, like geographers, CNRS research workers, geologits, architects, designers and developers. After gathering all kinds of information, I sorted them and kept what was the most important. You will see below some examples of my discoveries and the conclusions that I made from them.

Some definitions and numbers.

Knowing the exact meaning of a word and what it will mean for other people, is imperative. Especially when it comes to the words of your subject. It is also important to learn information related to them and depending on your context.

  • * Resilience = Bouncing back from a crisis
  • * Storm = Significant atmospheric disturbances on land or sea
  • * Coastline = The land along the coast

Metropolitan France is composed of 5,500 km coastlines, which represents 6% of the territory and where 12% of the population lives.

Beaufort wind force scale.

The most commun scale to index the winds is Beaufort wind force scale and it classifies the winds according to their effects both on land and at sea. Beaufort scale includes thirteen levels, going from calm (0) to hurricane (12). The observations we can make can go from a perfect mirror sea to a complete devastation. Hence the importance of knowing these surrounding winds which are invisible but yet can turn out to be extremely violent.

12 Hurricane (more than 118 km/h)

11 Violent storm (103 to 117 km/h)

10 Storm (89 to 102 km/h)

9 Strong gale (75 to 88 km/h)

8 Gale (62 to 74 km/h)

7 Moderate gale (50 to 61 km/h)

6 Strong breeze (39 to 49 km/h)

5 Fresh breeze (29 to 38 km/h)

4 Moderate breeze (20 to 28 km/h)

3 Gentle breeze (12 to 19 km/h)

2 Light breeze (6 to 11 km/h)

1 Light air (1 to 5 km/h)

0 Calm (less then 1 km)

As my subject being on the storms, I decided to expand my research on them plus the winds above it (violent storms and hurricanes). When these winds rage, trees are uprooted, infrastructures suffer considerable damage, big flooding and power cuts can happen.

Study of the PPRL. (translation: Coastal Risk Prevention Plan)

I read the PPRL of Lesconil and the surrounding areas. I discovered several risk zones around Lesconil and I created a map to highlight them.

Campings are very fragile habitats and they are all located in flood hazard or flood risk areas which make them very vulnerable against the wind. Furthermore, the coastlines which are the barriers between the sea and the land, are very eroded due to several storms. Located nearby the campings and the flood risk areas, they can become very dangerous.

02. Creation of a design tool

As my interviews and the existing tools weren’t enough to gather all the information I needed, I created my own design tool: a card game. The purpose of this game is to know what knowledge people have exactly on the winds and Beaufort scale (as the terms are used on the news). I created thirteen cards with numbers going from 0 to 12, representing the thirteen different winds, and I did the same for the name of the winds, their speeds and their consequences on sea and on land. When I interviewed users, I drew one wind name and they had to find the four other related cards.

Game 1.

I drow one card with a wind name and the user has to find the right related cards, so its speed, its consequences on sea and on land, and its number on Beaufort scale.

Margaux. Casual tourist

L’Escale Café boss. Permanent resident

Volunteer firefighter. Permanent resident

Bistrot du Port waitress. Permanent resident

Inferences.

I did this game with twenty people and neither of them had all the good answers: 40% had no good answers, 20% had one correct card, 40% had two correct cards. That means that people, even if they thought they had, don’t have good knowledge at all of the winds.

Game 2.

I printed some pictures of a storm. People have to find out what kind of wind it is and the four other related cards. Same conclusion as the other game, people didn’t get the right answers.

Inferences of phase 1

  • Three types of people : permanent residents, seasonal inhabitants and casual tourists. Permanent residents feel that they know the wind, but it has changed over the years and they might not be all aware of it. Seasonal inhabitants are increasing every year and most of them are less aware of the wind and its consequences.
  • People have issues visualizing and understanding Beaufort scale, so they don’t understand the potential dangers.
  • Bad risk culture and wrong move when people have to react during a crisis.
  • Fascination with the risk that makes it even more dangerous.
  • Risk minimization.
  • Very little awareness raised.

Phase 3. Development of Wind Universe

I chose to call my project Wind Universe (from the third concept) because people can clearly understand the name and what it is about.

Reminders

01. Location: Sémaphoren

The Sémaphore is located in at strategic location (cf. Poster Wind Universe), it is also out of a risk area (cf. PPRL). Built in 1804, it used to warn sailors of any strong gale and it proved its sustainability. Renovated in 2000 by the city hall to create offices, it was turned into an exhibition place in 2010.

01. Interactive projections on facades

People’s attention is drawn to a movement taking place onto the Sémaphore facades. Intrigued, they come closer and discover that old storms are projected. On the other facade, they can interact with the mapping though sensors and have information on real-time about today’s winds in the world.

Goals.
  • Draw attention to the building
  • Make people want to come inside to discover more about this universe.

02. Immersive experience

In the first on the left, Wind Universe experience takes place. A wooden interactive model representing Lesconil coastlines is displayed on an adjustable high table. A leap motion is integrated inside this model to catch user’s hand movement. On the screen before the user, a representation of Lesconil coastlines appears and depending of his moves, it will show him the damage that could be done. The other room on the right is dedicated to a local artist.

The user’s move represents the wind’s move.

  • Appropriate the wind by the move
  • Understand damage caused by the wind by experimenting it
  • Be safe during this experience

03. Identification of the Sémaphore as a refuge

Components inside the two rooms highlight the fact that the Sémaphore can be turned into a refuge. It will make users understand that they can go in there if they are surprised by a storm or are located in the campings nearby and have a flood.

Goals.
  • Identify the place as a refuge
  • Conceive an adjustable space for users to turn it easily into a refuge

In the exhibition room for local artists, users can be drawn to some components such as hidden scales, form depth, dissimulated handles.

Use scenario.

They visit the other exhibition from a local artist.

They discover hidden components that show them it is a refuge.

What happens when there is a storm?

The refuge is made for 14 people. Usually striking during Winter, there are not so many people around in Lesconil. If it happens during Summer, the most fragile people can be moved here while the other will have to wait for the firefighter to drive them to the omnisports club few kilometers away (that is usually what they do). People will have a protocol at their disposal to show them how to use the space. I created a bathroom and a kitchenette in the former offices which are daily hidden to the public by sliding panels. Users will have everything that they need and I try to keep their intimicy with integrated curtains for the beds, customizable panel in the bathroom. The Wind Universe room will be a socialization space and the other room will be the space to eat and sleep.

Layout of the space when it is a refuge.

Socialization space.

When there is a storm, people feel fear and they need to be in a friendly atmosphere. They can shift the acoustic panels to create separated spaces to discuss with other people. They can pull some seats from below the screen and place them as they wish.

Rendering of socialization area.

Living room space.

Users can unfold bunk beds and the tables from the wall that used to be structure to hang pictures or installations. They can also deployed chairs which are stored into the cupboard in the other room and replaced them by artistic installations. Underneath the bunk beds, they have an open push drawer with a pillow and a sleeping bag. They also have some tools that they can plug onto the panel in the bathroom to customize it and for the cleanliness.
